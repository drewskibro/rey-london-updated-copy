Why the Flu Jab Matters More Than You Think

Every year, influenza hospitalises thousands and kills an average of 11,000 people in England alone. It's not just a bad cold — for vulnerable groups, it can be life-threatening. And even for healthy adults, a bout of flu typically means 1–2 weeks off work, high fevers, and prolonged fatigue.

The flu vaccine is the single most effective way to protect yourself and the people around you. It typically reduces your risk of catching flu by 40–60% in a matched season, and significantly reduces the severity if you do catch it.

Who Qualifies for a Free NHS Flu Jab?

The NHS offers free flu vaccination to people at higher risk of complications. For the 2025/26 season, eligible groups include:

  • Everyone aged 65 and over
  • Pregnant women — at any stage of pregnancy
  • People with long-term conditions: asthma (requiring a steroid inhaler or tablets), diabetes, chronic heart disease, chronic kidney disease, chronic liver disease, COPD, neurological conditions, weakened immune systems
  • People living with someone who is immunocompromised
  • Carers — who receive a carer's allowance or are the main carer for a vulnerable person
  • Frontline health and social care workers
  • Children aged 2–17 — via the nasal spray vaccine (usually given at school or GP surgeries)
  • People in residential or nursing homes
  • People with a BMI of 40 or above

If you're in any of these groups, you can get your free flu jab at REY London — no appointment needed. Just bring your NHS exemption evidence or let our pharmacist check your eligibility.

The Private Option: Why Healthy Adults Should Consider It Too

If you don't qualify for a free NHS jab, a private flu vaccination is one of the best £14.99 you'll spend all year. Here's why:

  • Protect your family — Flu spreads through households rapidly. If you have young children, elderly parents, or anyone vulnerable at home, your vaccination protects them too.
  • Avoid work disruption — Average flu illness lasts 7–14 days. If you're self-employed or can't afford time off, the vaccine is a no-brainer.
  • Reduce NHS pressure — Fewer flu cases means fewer A&E visits, fewer GP appointments, and better care for everyone.
  • Quick and convenient — Walk in, roll up your sleeve, done in 5 minutes. No GP appointment required.

When Is the Best Time to Get Vaccinated?

The flu season in the UK typically runs from December to March, peaking in January and February. The vaccine takes around 2 weeks to reach full effectiveness, so timing matters:

  • Ideal window: September to early November — maximum protection for the peak season
  • Still worthwhile: December through February — even mid-season vaccination provides benefit
  • Too late? Almost never. If flu is still circulating (which it often is into March/April), getting vaccinated is still better than not

"People often say 'I'll get it next week' and then it's January and they've already had the flu. The best time to get vaccinated is always now — not later."

— Sumeet Banker, Lead Pharmacist, REY London

Side Effects and Common Myths

Common Side Effects (Mild, Short-Lived)

  • Sore arm — The most common side effect. Usually resolves within 1–2 days.
  • Feeling slightly unwell — Low-grade headache, mild fatigue, or muscle aches for 24–48 hours. This is your immune system responding — it's a good sign.
  • Mild fever — Rare, but can occur. It's not the flu — it's your immune system building protection.

Myths We Hear Every Season

"The flu jab gave me the flu" — No. The injected vaccine contains inactivated virus fragments. It is physically impossible for it to cause influenza. If you caught something around the same time, it was coincidence or a different respiratory virus.

"I'm healthy, I don't need it" — Healthy adults absolutely can (and do) get severely ill with flu. And even mild cases spread to vulnerable people around you.

"I had it last year, I'm still protected" — No. Flu viruses mutate each year. This year's vaccine targets different strains.
