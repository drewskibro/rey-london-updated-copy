When to Start Planning Your Travel Health

The single biggest mistake we see at REY London? Patients booking their travel health appointments the week before they fly. Some vaccines need multiple doses spread over several weeks, and antimalarials should ideally be started before departure to check for side effects.

Here's our recommended timeline:

  • 8+ weeks before departure: Book your travel health consultation. This gives time for multi-dose vaccines like Hepatitis B (3 doses) and Rabies (3 doses).
  • 6 weeks before: Start any required multi-dose courses. Get your Yellow Fever jab if needed (single dose, valid for life).
  • 4 weeks before: Single-dose vaccines (Hepatitis A, Typhoid) can still be given and provide protection in time.
  • 2 weeks before: Start antimalarials if prescribed. Malarone starts 1–2 days before travel; Doxycycline starts 1–2 days before; Mefloquine starts 2–3 weeks before.
  • 1 week before: Assemble your travel health kit. Check your insurance documents.

"I tell every patient the same thing: the earlier you come in, the more options we have. Last-minute doesn't mean unprotected — we have accelerated schedules — but earlier is always better."

— Sumeet Banker, Lead Pharmacist, REY London

Essential Vaccinations: What You Might Need

Not every traveller needs every vaccine. Your specific requirements depend on your destination, duration, activities, and medical history. Here's what we typically assess:

Routine Vaccines (Check You're Up to Date)

  • MMR — Measles, Mumps, and Rubella. If you were born after 1970 and haven't had two doses, you may need a booster.
  • Tetanus/Diphtheria/Polio — Booster recommended every 10 years for travellers, especially to developing countries.
  • COVID-19 — Stay up to date with current guidance, particularly for destinations with entry requirements.

Travel-Specific Vaccines

  • Hepatitis A — Recommended for most destinations outside Western Europe, North America, and Australasia. Single dose gives protection for 1 year; booster at 6–12 months for lifelong cover. Learn more about Hepatitis vaccines →
  • Hepatitis B — Important for longer stays, healthcare workers, or travellers likely to have medical treatment abroad. 3-dose course.
  • Typhoid — Essential for South Asia, South East Asia, Africa, and Central/South America. Single injection, valid for 3 years.
  • Yellow Fever — Required (not just recommended) for entry to many African and South American countries. We're a NaTHNaC-registered Yellow Fever centre and can issue the official ICVP certificate.
  • Rabies — Pre-exposure course recommended for backpackers, animal workers, cyclists, and travellers to remote areas where post-bite treatment may be hard to access.
  • Japanese Encephalitis — For rural travellers to South East Asia and the Far East during the monsoon season.
  • Meningitis ACWY — Required for Hajj/Umrah pilgrims; recommended for sub-Saharan Africa's "meningitis belt".

Malaria Prevention

Malaria kills over 600,000 people per year worldwide. It's transmitted by mosquitoes, most commonly in sub-Saharan Africa, South East Asia, and parts of South America. There is no vaccine routinely available for adult travellers — prevention relies on tablets and bite avoidance.

Antimalarial Options We Prescribe

  • Malarone (Atovaquone/Proguanil) — Start 1–2 days before travel, continue for 7 days after return. Fewest side effects, best tolerated. Our most popular choice.
  • Doxycycline — Start 1–2 days before, continue for 4 weeks after return. Budget-friendly. Can cause sun sensitivity.
  • Mefloquine (Lariam) — Weekly dosing. Start 2–3 weeks before. Suitable for longer trips but has a higher side-effect profile. Not for everyone.

Bite Prevention (ABCD Approach)

  • Awareness — Know if your destination is a malaria risk area
  • Bite prevention — Use DEET-based repellent (50%+), sleep under treated nets, wear long sleeves/trousers at dusk
  • Chemoprophylaxis — Take antimalarials as prescribed, every dose, on time
  • Diagnosis — Seek urgent medical attention if you develop a fever within 12 months of returning from a malaria area
Important: Post-Travel Fever

If you develop a fever, flu-like illness, or feel unwell within 12 months of returning from a malaria-risk area — even if you took antimalarials — seek urgent medical attention and tell the doctor you've been to a malaria area. Early diagnosis saves lives.

Your Travel Health Kit: What to Pack

A well-stocked travel health kit can be the difference between a minor inconvenience and a ruined trip. Here's what our pharmacists recommend:

Essentials (Every Trip)

  • Prescription medications — in original packaging with pharmacy labels. Carry a GP or pharmacist letter for controlled drugs.
  • Insect repellent — at least 50% DEET for malaria areas, or 20% DEET/Picaridin for lower-risk destinations.
  • Broad-spectrum sunscreen — SPF 30+ minimum, reapply every 2 hours.
  • Oral rehydration sachets — essential for managing traveller's diarrhoea and heat-related dehydration.
  • Paracetamol and ibuprofen — for pain, fever, and inflammation.
  • Plasters, antiseptic wipes, and wound closure strips — minor cuts in tropical climates can quickly become infected.
  • Antihistamines — for insect bites, allergic reactions, and motion sickness (certain types).

For Tropical/Adventure Destinations

  • Mosquito net (permethrin-treated)
  • Water purification tablets
  • Loperamide (Imodium) for acute diarrhoea
  • Hydrocortisone cream for bites and rashes
  • Thermometer

Insurance & Documents

Health preparations mean nothing if you can't access care abroad. Before you go:

  • Travel insurance with medical cover — Ensure it covers your destination, activities (trekking, diving, etc.), and a minimum of £2 million medical expenses including repatriation.
  • GHIC / EHIC — For European destinations. Gives access to state-provided healthcare. Not a substitute for travel insurance.
  • Yellow Fever Certificate (ICVP) — Required for entry to many countries. Must be the official WHO-standardised document issued by a registered centre.
  • Vaccination record — Keep a copy of all travel vaccinations received. We provide a printed record at both locations.
  • GP letter — If you carry prescription medications, especially controlled drugs (e.g., strong painkillers, ADHD medication).

Destination-Specific Quick Guides

Every destination has different requirements. Here are quick guides for our most commonly requested regions:

Thailand

Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B, Typhoid, Tetanus booster. Consider Japanese Encephalitis for rural/long stays. Antimalarials for border regions only (not Bangkok or beach resorts). See our full Thailand guide →

Sub-Saharan Africa

Yellow Fever (often mandatory), Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Meningitis ACWY (for the meningitis belt), Rabies for remote travel. Antimalarials essential for most countries.

India & South Asia

Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid (high risk), Tetanus, Rabies (strongly recommended — stray animals are common). Antimalarials for most regions outside major cities.

South America

Yellow Fever (required for many countries and for onward travel to other destinations), Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Rabies for Amazon/rural areas. Antimalarials for Amazon basin regions.
