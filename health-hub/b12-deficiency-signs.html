7 Signs You Might Be Deficient in Vitamin B12

What Is Vitamin B12 and Why Does It Matter?

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is essential for making red blood cells, maintaining the nervous system, and synthesising DNA. Your body cannot produce it — you must get it from food or supplements. And here's the catch: deficiency develops slowly, over months or even years, meaning many people are walking around with low levels without realising it.

In the UK, an estimated 6% of adults under 60 and up to 20% of adults over 60 have sub-optimal B12 levels. Among vegans and vegetarians, the figure may be as high as 50%.

1. Crushing, Unexplained Fatigue

This isn't just "feeling tired". B12-deficiency fatigue is a deep, bone-level exhaustion that sleep doesn't fix. Your body can't make enough healthy red blood cells to carry oxygen efficiently, so every cell in your body is essentially running on fumes.

What to look for: Tiredness that persists despite adequate sleep. Feeling like you need to nap every afternoon. A general sense of heaviness in your limbs.

2. Brain Fog and Memory Problems

B12 plays a critical role in nerve function and neurotransmitter production. When levels drop, you may notice difficulty concentrating, trouble finding the right word, forgetting appointments, or a general feeling that your thinking is "fuzzy".

In older adults, this can sometimes be mistaken for early dementia — which is why getting tested is so important.

3. Pins and Needles (Paraesthesia)

Tingling or numbness in your hands and feet is a classic neurological symptom of B12 deficiency. B12 is essential for producing myelin, the protective sheath around your nerves. Without enough B12, myelin degrades and nerve signals become disrupted.

Don't Ignore Neurological Symptoms

Persistent tingling, numbness, or difficulty walking can indicate nerve damage from prolonged B12 deficiency. If left untreated, some neurological damage can become permanent. See a pharmacist or GP promptly if you're experiencing these symptoms.

4. Mood Changes and Depression

B12 is involved in producing serotonin — the "feel-good" neurotransmitter. Low levels have been linked to increased risk of depression, anxiety, and irritability. If mood changes have no obvious cause, B12 deficiency is worth investigating.

5. Pale or Yellowish Skin

Without adequate B12, your body produces abnormally large, fragile red blood cells (megaloblasts) that break down more easily. This can cause a pale complexion or a slight yellowish tinge (jaundice) as bilirubin — a byproduct of red blood cell breakdown — accumulates.

6. Mouth Ulcers and Glossitis

A swollen, inflamed, red tongue (glossitis) is a surprisingly common sign of B12 deficiency. You might also notice frequent mouth ulcers, a burning sensation on the tongue, or changes in how food tastes.

7. Dizziness and Breathlessness

As anaemia develops from B12 deficiency, your body struggles to transport enough oxygen. This can manifest as dizziness on standing, shortness of breath during mild exertion (climbing stairs, carrying shopping), and an elevated heart rate.

Who Is Most at Risk?

Certain groups are significantly more likely to develop B12 deficiency:

  • Vegans and vegetarians — B12 occurs naturally almost exclusively in animal products (meat, fish, eggs, dairy).
  • Adults over 50 — Stomach acid production declines with age, reducing B12 absorption from food.
  • People on metformin — This common diabetes medication can reduce B12 absorption by up to 30%.
  • People on proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) — Omeprazole, lansoprazole, and similar drugs reduce stomach acid needed to release B12 from food.
  • Those with gut conditions — Crohn's disease, coeliac disease, and other conditions affecting the ileum (where B12 is absorbed).
  • People with pernicious anaemia — An autoimmune condition where the body attacks intrinsic factor, a protein needed to absorb B12.

B12 Injections vs. Tablets: When an Injection Is the Better Choice

Over-the-counter B12 tablets and sprays are readily available. So when does an injection make more sense?

Injections are recommended when:

  • You have confirmed pernicious anaemia (oral B12 won't be absorbed)
  • You're taking medications that impair absorption (metformin, PPIs)
  • You have gut conditions affecting the ileum
  • You have neurological symptoms (tingling, numbness, cognitive changes) — these need rapid correction
  • You want guaranteed, measurable results — 100% of the injected dose reaches your bloodstream

"I see patients every week who've been taking oral B12 supplements for months with no improvement. Once we switch to injections, they often feel the difference within 48 hours. If your gut can't absorb it, swallowing more of it won't help."

— Sumeet Banker, Lead Pharmacist, REY London
